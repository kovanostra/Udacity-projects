Data description¶

For this project I analyzed the dataset of the Titanic's passengers to uncover correlations between some of their characteristics and factors that made them more likely to survive. The dataset of the Titanic's passengers contains 12 different types of data for each of them. Below each of the data types is presented in the order it appears in the csv file:

  1. 'PassengerId' - Passenger ID (1 to 891)
  2. 'Survived' - Survived or not (1 and 0 respectively)
  3. 'Pclass' - Passenger class $(1^{st}, 2^{nd}$ or $3^{rd})$
  4. 'Name' - Passenger name (If female the name is in parenthesis after the husband's name)
  5. 'Sex' - Passenger sex
  6. 'Age' - Passenger age
  7. 'SibSp' - Siblings or Spouses
  8. 'Parch' - Parents or Children
  9. 'Ticket' - Ticket number
  10. 'Fare' - Ticket fare
  11. 'Cabin' - Cabin number
  12. 'Embarked' - Port of embarkement (Cherbourg (C), Queenstown (Q) or Southampton (S))

Methods¶

I analyzed whether the following characteristics affected the passengers' survival rates:

1. The passengers' sex
2. The passengers' age characteristics
3. Whether the passenger was a child. If yes, was it accompanied by his/her parent(s) or not
4. How the likelihood to die was affected by the class of the ticket for males and females

Due to evacuation priorities men and women are expected to have different likelihood of survival. Thus I split the data and the analysis between men and women for all questions apart from the third. As children I selected all those of less than 16 years old. I made this choice because of one passenger who was 16 years old with Pchild value equal to 3. This passenger was obviously both parent and child and thus I excluded him/her from that part of the analysis.

Cleaning the data¶

In our data there are various missing points, for example in the 'Age' and 'Cabin' columns there are 177 and 687 NaN values respectively. I didn't analyse any data requiring the 'Cabin' column due to the fact that about 77% of the data are missing. However, about 80% of the passengers ages are present, which made possible to analyze it. Since no assumptions could be made in order to impute the missing data, I ignored them and worked with the 80% of the dataset for that analysis. I used the pandas method 'dropna()' to ignore the NaN, when necessary.

Discussion¶

From the distributions of the ages of the passengers we can see that the majority of passengers were between 17 and 40 years old along with a lot of very young kids.

The distributions of women who survived or died seem to be very similar. This shows that the age wasn't an important factor determining whether a woman would survive or not. As seen in the histogram, women of age between 7 and 10 only survived and between 33 and 36 only died. This is likely to be due to the fact that some of the data of the ages are missing from our dataset or because this dataset pertains to 891 out of the estimated 2,200 passengers of the Titanic.

Finally, from the distributions of the ages of the men on board, we can see that those from 30 years old and above had about the same chance to survive or die. Kids were more likely to die, despite the evacuation priorities, and those between 15 and 28 were more likely to survive than all the rest.

Discussion¶

From the previous bar plot we can see that unacompanied children were more than twice more likely to survive than die, while being accompanied by parents made them less and less likely to survive. This is a counterintuitive result because one would expect that parents would help children evacuate. However, it seems that being without a parent on the boat somehow helped children to escape it more easily than the rest of them.

Discussion¶

The last two bar plots show a lot about the economic inequalities among those who survived. First of all, women were evacuated first and thus 74.2% of them managed to survive. However, more than 90% of the women in first and second class survived, while only 50% of those in third class survived. Thus during evacuation, we can see that there were a clear preference to those of middle to upper class. Moreover, a similar effect is seen among men. Even though 81.1% of the men died, about 38% of those in first class managed to survive, where only 14% and 17% of them managed to survive respectively in the second and third class.

Conclusion¶

To sum up, the majority of losses in the Titanic came from the male population on board and especially from those of middle to lower class. Men coming from the higher class had clearly more chances to survive than the rest. Almost all women of middle to higher class survived the sink, while those of lower class had equal chances to survive or die. Among children, those that were without any parents were more than twice more likely to survive than die, where the rest had less chances with growing number of parents along them. Finally, it is imprortant to notice that this dataset contains data for 891 out of the about 2,200 passengers of the Titanic. Thus any results from analyzing them, without further knowledge on whether these data are representative of the population or not, should be taken with a grain of salt.
